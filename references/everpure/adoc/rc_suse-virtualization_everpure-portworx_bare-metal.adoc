Enterprises are under pressure to modernize IT infrastructure while maintaining high levels of performance, resilience, and cost efficiency.
Deploying {svirt-long} with {pxe-long} and {pfa-long} on bare metal helps address these challenges by providing a scalable and reliable foundation for virtualized workloads.
This integrated solution reduces operational complexity, protects critical data, accelerates workload deployment, and maximizes infrastructure investments, enabling organizations to support digital transformation with confidence.


=== Business problem

Enterprises are facing increasing challenges in managing modern, data-intensive applications while supporting diverse infrastructure needs across data centers, edge sites, and hybrid environments. Traditional virtualization and storage approaches often lack the agility, scalability, and resilience required for today’s workloads, creating bottlenecks that affect multiple stakeholders.

* *Developers* struggle with slow provisioning and lack of persistent storage for stateful applications.

* *IT operators and platform engineers* face complexity in managing storage, ensuring high availability, and handling disaster recovery across distributed environments.

* *Line of business managers* are impacted by delays in application delivery, leading to missed opportunities and reduced competitiveness.

* *C-suite executives* must balance modernization with cost efficiency, risk reduction, and alignment to digital transformation goals.

These challenges highlight the need for an integrated solution that simplifies operations, enhances resilience, and delivers enterprise-grade scalability for virtualized and cloud-native workloads.



=== Business value

By addressing the complexity, scalability, and resiliency limitations of traditional virtualization and storage, {svirt} with {pxe} and {pfa-long} on bare metal empowers organizations to streamline operations while preparing for future growth.
Developers gain faster access to persistent storage, enabling rapid innovation and accelerated application delivery.
IT operators and platform engineers benefit from simplified management, automated scaling, and built-in data protection, all of which significantly reduce operational overhead.

{svirt} enhances infrastructure efficiency by supporting both container and virtual machine workloads on the same platform.
This unified approach minimizes infrastructure sprawl, optimizes resource utilization, and simplifies operations across diverse environments.

For business leaders, these capabilities translate into improved agility, faster time-to-market, and stronger returns on infrastructure investments, while also reducing overall business risk.
Together, {svirt} and {px-long} provide a robust foundation not only for current enterprise workloads but also for forward-looking initiatives such as hybrid cloud expansion, edge computing, and digital transformation.

== Design considerations


The diagram below shows a high-level, reference design for {svirt} integrated with {pxe} deployed on bare-metal nodes along with a {pfa-long}.
By implementing this design, a platform engineering team can automate the provisioning of a well-defined architecture following best practices that includes high availability, operations management, observability, business continuity, performance, and security.

image::suse-portworx-arc-diagram.png[title="Reference Design", Reference Design, align="center", width=90%]


The foundation of the deployment is the bare metal compute nodes that will host both the {svirt} hypervisor and the {px} storage layer.



=== Cluster topology and sizing

* *Node count*: While Portworx requires a minimum of three storage nodes to establish quorum, six nodes are recommended for production environments.

* *Capacity impact*: In a six-node cluster, losing one node impacts only one-sixth of the total capacity.

* *I/O distribution*: A six-node cluster distributes I/O requests more effectively.

* *Fault domains*: Ensure the cluster spans multiple availability zones or racks.


=== Resource requirements per node

* *CPU and memory*: Each node requires a minimum of 8 CPU cores and 32 GB of RAM to ensure stable {px} operation alongside virtualized workloads.

* *Role assignment:* Label all nodes contributing storage with `portworx.io/node-type=storage`.
This allows the operator to automatically discover and provision devices.


=== Network

Performance in a hyper-converged environment relies heavily on network stability and bandwidth. The network must be designed to handle both management traffic and high-volume storage replication.

* *Interface configuration*

** *Traffic separation:* Assign dedicated network interfaces (NICs) for Management Traffic (Kubernetes control plane) and Data Traffic ({px} replication).
Isolating data traffic prevents storage replication from congesting the control plane.

** *Redundancy:* Use NIC bonding (mode 4 or LACP) to ensure high availability and load balancing across interfaces.


* *Performance tuning*

* *Bandwidth*: A minimum of 10 Gbps is required.
However, for production virtualization workloads, 25 Gbps or higher is strongly recommended to lower write latency and improve replication efficiency.

* *Jumbo Frames*: Configure MTU 9000 (Jumbo Frames) on the storage network interfaces and physical switches to improve throughput for iSCSI and replication traffic.


=== Storage

* *Physical storage (Backing Disks)*: {px} requires raw block devices to provide persistent storage.
In this architecture, these devices are provisioned from the {pfa-long}.


* *Storage connectivity*
+
--
* *Protocols:* Block devices can be presented via Fibre Channel (FC) or iSCSI.

** *Fibre Channel:* Ensure proper zoning in the SAN fabric.

** *iSCSI:* Dedicate Ethernet interfaces solely for storage traffic to avoid contention.

* *Multipathing:* Configure multipathing on the {svirt} nodes.
This provides redundancy in case of a path failure and can deliver additional bandwidth.
--

* *Volume presentation*: Present only *one volume per storage node* from the {pfa-long}.
This simplifies management, as the storage pool can be resized by expanding this backing disk without triggering expensive data rebalancing operations.


* *Storage pools and I/O profiling*: {px} groups drives with similar performance characteristics into storage pools.
In a bare metal environment using an all-flash array, ensure *consistent specifications for all backing disks* to maintain uniform performance across the cluster.

* *Journaling*: {px} uses a journal device for metadata writes.
You should allocate a 3 GB volume for the journal.
And, because metadata writes are frequent, this volume must be on high-performance storage (SSD/NVMe).
+
[NOTE]
====
When using {pfa-long} storage, provision a specific 3 GB volume for this purpose to ensure metadata throughput does not become a bottleneck.
====


* *Key-Value database (KVDB)*

** *Internal KVDB:* For most deployments, the internal KVDB is recommended.
It eliminates the need for managing an external etcd cluster.

** *Dedicated Resources:* Assign a dedicated 64 GB disk on three specific nodes for the KVDB.
This isolates metadata I/O from application storage traffic.
Label these nodes `px/metadata-node=true` and ensure they are distributed across fault domains.



== Preparing the {svirt-long} environment

Before deploying {px}, you must prepare the {svirt} platform and underlying nodes to ensure they meet the specific storage requirements.
Review the following configurations to ensure a stable and performant integration.


=== Cluster topology and sizing

To ensure high availability and data redundancy, the physical layout of the cluster must be planned carefully:

* *Node Count*: While a minimum of three storage nodes is required to establish quorum, six storage nodes are recommended for production environments.
This reduces the impact of node failures and improves I/O load distribution.

* *Capacity Impact*: In a six-node cluster, losing a single node affects only one-sixth of the total capacity, allowing the remaining nodes to rebalance workloads more efficiently.

* *I/O Distribution*: A larger cluster spreads I/O requests more evenly, helping reduce latency during peak traffic or rebuild operations.

* *Fault Domains*: Distribute storage nodes evenly across availability zones, racks, or host groups to protect against localized hardware failures.

* *Anti-Affinity*: When running {svirt-long} on top of another hypervisor (for example, KVM), configure anti-affinity rules to ensure {px} storage nodes do not share the same physical host.


=== Node resource requirements

Each node participating in the storage cluster must meet the following minimum requirements:

* *CPU and Memory*: Allocate at least 8 vCPUs and 32 GB of RAM per node to support Portworx services alongside virtual machine workloads.
* *Dedicated Storage Devices*: Provide raw block devices on each storage node. These devices must not be formatted or managed by LVM or filesystems, as Portworx requires direct disk access.
* *Platform Consistency*: Ensure all nodes run the same SUSE Linux Enterprise Server version and kernel to avoid compatibility issues.


=== Operating system and network prerequisites

Proper base system configuration is required for reliable storage clustering:

* *Time Synchronization*: Configure NTP on all nodes to maintain cluster quorum and metadata consistency.

* *Name Resolution*: Ensure all nodes can resolve each other’s hostnames using DNS or `/etc/hosts`.

* *Swap Configuration*: Disable swap on storage nodes. If swap cannot be disabled, {px} must be explicitly configured to tolerate it.

* *Security Policies*: Update firewall rules and SELinux or AppArmor profiles to allow required {px} communication ports.

* *Network Connectivity*: Verify full bidirectional connectivity between all nodes on both management and data networks.


=== Node labeling and role assignment

{px} relies on Kubernetes labels to determine which nodes contribute storage resources:

* *Storage Nodes*: Apply the label `portworx.io/node-type=storage` to all nodes that provide storage.
This allows the {pxo} to automatically discover and provision devices on these specific nodes.

* *Storageless Nodes*: For compute-only nodes, do not apply the storage label.
In hyperconverged environments where every node provides storage, the label is optional but recommended for clarity.


=== Considerations for {svirt-long}

{svirt} provides a consistent and flexible experience across deployment models, including bare metal and public or private cloud environments, while allowing customization to meet specific infrastructure needs.

Subscription handling varies based on deployment type.
Virtualized environments typically use vCPU- or VM-based subscriptions, while bare-metal deployments are socket-based, with each subscription covering a defined number of physical sockets per host.

A key advantage of {svirt} is its tight integration with {srancher}.
This enables virtual machines and containerized workloads to run side by side in a unified, cloud-native platform, simplifying management and supporting hybrid and edge computing use cases.


==== Networking

A well-designed network is critical for performance and reliability when running {px} on {svirt}.

Carefully plan management and data networks to ensure efficient communication between nodes and consistent data availability.
Firewall configuration and VLAN segmentation should be used to enhance security and isolate traffic where required.

By following these networking best practices, you can improve cluster stability, optimize storage performance, and ensure high availability across the {px} deployment.



==== Management and data interfaces

When planning network configurations for {px} on {svirt}, the following best practices help ensure efficient data flow, scalability, and predictable performance.


* *Dedicated network interfaces*: Assign separate virtual or physical network interfaces for management and data traffic.
+
--
* Management traffic includes cluster operations and control-plane communications.
* Data traffic includes storage I/O, volume access, and replication.
--
+
Isolating these traffic types helps prevent congestion and avoids performance degradation during peak load or rebuild operations.


* *Bridge or MacvTap interfaces*: In KVM-based {svirt} environments, configure Linux bridges or MacvTap interfaces to provide direct network access to {px} nodes.
This approach reduces latency and improves throughput for data replication and I/O-heavy workloads.


* *Consistent network configuration across hosts*: Ensure that all {svirt} hosts use consistent:
+
--
* Interface naming conventions
* IP addressing schemes
* VLAN assignments
--
+
Consistency reduces operational complexity and prevents issues during scaling, failover, or node migration.

* *Jumbo frames and MTU settings*: For high-throughput storage workloads, enable jumbo frames by setting MTU to 9000 bytes on data interfaces.
Jumbo frames should only be enabled if the entire network path supports the configured MTU to avoid packet fragmentation or dropped traffic.

* *Bandwidth and quality of service*: Reserve sufficient network bandwidth for {px} data traffic.
When networks are shared, implement Quality of Service (QoS) policies to ensure predictable latency and throughput for storage replication and I/O.

* *Redundancy and bonding*: Use NIC bonding or teaming to provide redundancy and load balancing across both management and data networks.
Bonding mode 4 (802.3ad or LACP) is recommended.
This configuration improves fault tolerance and ensures continued operation during NIC or link failures.


* *Firewall and security rules*:
+
--
* Configure firewall policies to allow required {px} communication ports for control, data, and API traffic between nodes.

* Restrict external access to management interfaces to secure administrative operations and reduce attack surface.
--

* *DNS and time synchronization*: Verify that all nodes have:
+
--
* Consistent DNS resolution

* Accurate NTP synchronization
--
+
{px} relies on stable name resolution and precise timekeeping to maintain cluster health and metadata consistency.

* *Default NIC usage*: By default, {px} uses the same network interfaces as the Kubernetes cluster for both management traffic and data replication.
This simplifies initial deployment and is suitable for:
+
--
* Lab environments

* Proof-of-concept setups

* Development clusters
--
+
However, this configuration can lead to congestion and should be avoided in performance-sensitive or production environments.
+
image::Portworx-default-network-configuration.png[title="{px} Default Network Configuration", {px} Default Network Configuration, align="center", width=90%]


* *Dedicated data networks*: For production deployments, {px} can be configured to use a dedicated NIC for data replication between nodes.
This separation ensures that:
+
--
* Storage replication traffic does not interfere with management or application traffic

* Kubernetes control-plane and workload networking remain uncongested
--
+
Dedicated data networks significantly improve throughput and reliability in clusters with high replication demands.
+
image::Portworx-dedicated-network-configuration.png[title="{px} Dedicated Data Network Configuration", {px} Dedicated Data Network Configuration, align="center", width=90%]


* *Network performance*: The physical network infrastructure has a direct impact on {px} write latency and overall storage throughput.
Because {px} replicates data to peer nodes before acknowledging write operations, both bandwidth and latency are critical factors.
Consider the following:
+
--
* Use NICs of the same speed and configuration for all storage nodes.

* Ensure appropriate bandwidth:
** Testing: 1 Gbps or higher
** Typical enterprise environments: 10 Gbps or higher
** High-performance environments: 25 Gbps or higher

* Ensure latency is less than 5 ms round-trip between storage nodes
--
+
Higher bandwidth and lower latency networks improve replication efficiency, reduce write latency, and enhance stability for data-intensive workloads.


== Backing disks for the Portworx Storage cluster

When configuring a {px} storage cluster on {svirt}, the choice and configuration of backing disks are critical for achieving optimal performance, reliability, and scalability.
The following considerations help guide the selection and configuration of backing disks for {px} deployments.


=== Block devices

{px} requires raw block storage devices as backing storage for the cluster.

* Each storage node must be provisioned with raw block devices.

* Devices must not be pre-formatted with file systems or managed by LVM.

* Block devices can be sourced from:

** Local disks on bare-metal worker nodes

** External storage arrays such as {pfa-long} or other SAN systems presenting block devices

* Ensure all backing devices are visible and accessible to the host operating system on each worker node.


=== Disk type and performance

The type of block devices used directly impacts capacity, I/O performance, and throughput.

* High-performance devices such as NVMe SSDs provide superior performance for Kubernetes workloads using {px} persistent volumes.

* SATA or SAS SSDs balance cost and performance.

* Traditional HDDs are supported but may limit performance for latency-sensitive workloads.

Select disk types based on application I/O requirements and performance expectations.


=== Disk redundancy and fault tolerance

{px} provides data protection through software-based replication.

* Application owners can define replication factors (for example, `repl2` or `repl3`) to store replicas across multiple nodes.
* Replication ensures data availability during node or disk failures.

Additional disk-level protection can be achieved through hardware RAID configurations:

* RAID1 or similar configurations can protect against single-disk failures within a node.
* This approach can reduce replica rebuild operations triggered by disk failures.
* Hardware RAID increases cost and should be evaluated against operational requirements.


=== Capacity planning

Backing disks define the total usable capacity of the {px} storage cluster.

* A small portion of disk space is reserved for {px} metadata and journaling.

* The majority of capacity is available for persistent volumes and their replicas.

* Effective capacity planning must account for:

** Application replication factors

** Expected growth in storage demand

** Performance headroom

Storage capacity can be expanded by resizing existing disks or adding additional block devices to storage nodes.


== Storage pools

A storage pool is a logical grouping of physical drives, which {px} uses to dynamically create virtual volumes.
You need a minimum of one drive per node to set up a storage pool.
Pools should consist of drives that share the same I/O priority and size, which will help simplify operations and ensure predictable and consistent performance across all nodes and replicas.

When you create a storage pool, {px} evaluates each drive through a benchmark process.
It categorizing the pool based on its throughput into one of three I/O priorities: low, medium, or high.
This allows you to align various applications with the appropriate tier of storage based on performance requirements.
For instance, database applications can be placed on flash devices for high performance, while applications managing logging data can utilize less performant options.

A single backing disk is required per node to create a storage pool.
However, for future scalability, consider the methods available to increase storage capacity in the cluster.
You can do this by expanding an existing disk (which makes more sense in the context of software-defined LUNs in a hardware array of physical disks) or by adding additional disks to the storage pool.

While both methods increase capacity, adding more disks necessitates an I/O rebalancing process that can be intensive.
Therefore, presenting a single backing disk for the storage cluster is recommended.

image::storage-cluster.png[title="Portworx Storage Pool Architecture", Portworx Storage Pool Architecture, align="center", width=90%]


{px} requires at least one storage pool per cluster to operate, but you can have multiple storage pools.

Multiple storage pools can be useful to:

** Separate workloads

** Build performance-based storage tiers

** Isolate different disk types


By carefully planning backing disks and storage pool configurations, {px} can deliver reliable, high-performance, and scalable persistent storage to {svirt} for both virtualized and containerized workloads.



== Journal devices

Portworx recommends configuring a journal device to handle metadata writes.
Journal writes are small and frequent; therefore, using a solid-state drive (SSD) or NVMe device is strongly recommended.

The journal device should have a capacity of 3 GB, as Portworx utilizes only this amount of space for journaling.
Allocating a larger device does not improve performance unless the increased capacity also provides additional IOPS, such as in many cloud environments.

The journal device must be at least as fast as the fastest storage device on the node participating in the Portworx storage cluster.
If the journal device performs slower, overall cluster write performance will be limited to the speed of the journal device.

=== Recommendations

- For nodes using local drives as backing disks, configure the journal device to be automatically created from existing disks rather than dedicating a separate physical disk for the 3 GB requirement.
This reduces hardware cost and complexity.

- For environments using storage arrays that can easily provision and present multiple volumes or LUNs to worker nodes, create a dedicated 3 GB volume specifically for the journal device to achieve better metadata write performance.

[NOTE]
====
Using a high-performance SSD or NVMe device for journaling significantly improves metadata throughput and overall I/O responsiveness in Portworx storage clusters deployed on SUSE Virtualization.
====

== Portworx KVDB

Portworx relies on a key-value database (KVDB) to store critical operational data such as cluster state, configuration, and volume metadata.
Because this information is essential to cluster operation, it must be highly available, resilient, and protected from node or device failures.

When deploying Portworx on {svirt-long}, you can configure the KVDB using one of the following options.

=== Internal KVDB

The internal KVDB is integrated directly into the Portworx deployment and is the recommended configuration for most environments.

This approach simplifies deployment by removing the need for an external database cluster, reducing management overhead and potential points of failure.
The internal KVDB provides a stable and efficient mechanism for maintaining cluster metadata and configuration state.

=== External etcd Cluster

Alternatively, Portworx can utilize an external etcd cluster as its KVDB.

This option is suitable for organizations that:

- Already maintain a production etcd infrastructure
- Require advanced features such as SyncDR (Synchronous Disaster Recovery)

SyncDR depends on an external etcd cluster to maintain data consistency and high availability across geographically distributed sites.
However, using an external etcd cluster introduces additional setup and management complexity.

When using this option, ensure the etcd cluster is deployed across multiple fault domains to maintain quorum during node or site outages.

=== Deployment recommendations

* Use an internal KVDB unless SyncDR or other advanced requirements dictate the use of an external etcd cluster.

* When using the internal KVDB, assign a dedicated 64 GB disk per KVDB node.
This isolates KVDB I/O operations from general storage traffic, reducing contention and latency for applications using Portworx volumes.

* The internal KVDB requires three SUSE Virtualization worker nodes for normal operation. The dedicated 64 GB disk is required only on these nodes.

* In case of a KVDB node failure, Portworx automatically promotes another node to act as a replacement KVDB node.

* Portworx recommends labeling six nodes as potential KVDB nodes using the following key-value pair `px/metadata-node=true`

These nodes should be distributed across multiple fault domains to ensure quorum and data availability even in the event of host or zone failures.

[NOTE]
====
Deploying KVDB on dedicated disks and distributing metadata nodes across fault domains enhances fault tolerance and ensures consistent performance in SUSE Virtualization environments.
====

== General third-party storage considerations

A hardware storage array can be used to provide the block devices as backing disks for the Portworx storage cluster.
Using a hardware storage array may offer benefits such as deduplication or compression for replicas stored in the Portworx storage cluster, depending on the capabilities of the storage array vendor.

When providing backing disks to Portworx storage nodes from a hardware array, special considerations should be taken.
This section outlines design considerations when using an external Storage Array Network for Portworx backing disks.

* Block devices are typically presented to physical hosts (SUSE Virtualization worker nodes) via Fibre-Channel or iSCSI connections.

* Portworx recommends using a minimum of two iSCSI or Fibre-Channel interfaces per node to present storage to the cluster.
A pair of interfaces provides high availability in the case of hardware failure, and with multi-pathing, can deliver additional bandwidth to the backing storage array.

Consider the configurations below for Fibre-Channel and iSCSI connections:

* *Fibre-Channel:* Ensure proper zoning in the SAN Fabric for the nodes.


* *iSCSI:* Dedicate Ethernet interfaces solely for storage traffic, rather than sharing them with SUSE Virtualization traffic.
Configure jumbo frames on these interfaces and any in-line network devices, including physical switches and the storage array, to enhance performance.


For storage cluster disks, Portworx recommends presenting a single volume per storage node based on your sizing decisions.
Using a single volume reduces performance impact if resizing operations are required later.
If your storage array supports volume resizing, these volumes can be expanded to increase the total storage of the Portworx storage cluster without triggering a rebalancing task, which is a costly storage operation involving data copying.

Creating additional volumes of a desired size is straightforward with an external hardware array.
In scenarios using an external hardware array, Portworx recommends creating 3 GB volumes from the array and presenting them to the worker nodes to be used as the journal device.

This reference configuration describes an architecture for deploying {svirt-long} with {pxe} by {pxe-provider} on bare metal infrastructure.
It supplements the existing SUSE and {pxe-provider} reference architecture by providing additional design considerations, deployment guidance, and operational best practices specific to virtualization and stateful workloads.

The solution integrates {svirt-long}, SUSE Rancher Prime, and {pxe} to deliver a unified platform for running virtual machines and container workloads on the same Kubernetes-based infrastructure.
{svirt-long} provides VM lifecycle management using KubeVirt, Rancher Prime offers centralized cluster and access management, and {pxe} delivers persistent storage services such as replication, failover, backup, and disaster recovery.

The reference design focuses on key architectural principles including high availability, scalability, performance, and operational consistency.
It outlines recommended cluster sizing, node resource requirements, fault-domain awareness, and network separation for management and data traffic.
Storage integration is based on raw block devices presented from {pxe-provider} FlashArray systems using Fibre Channel or iSCSI, with multipathing configured for resiliency and bandwidth.

Operational guidance covers storage pool design, journal device configuration, and KVDB deployment options.
The document recommends the use of a single backing disk per node for simplified capacity expansion, high-performance journal devices for metadata operations, and a highly available internal KVDB distributed across multiple nodes and fault domains.

By following the guidance in this document, organizations can deploy a stable and predictable {svirt} with Portworx storage on bare metal.
The architecture is designed to support production virtualization workloads while maintaining flexibility for future expansion into hybrid, edge, or mixed VM and container use cases.

== Conclusion
Organizations are modernizing their applications and infrastructure, transitioning from traditional, virtual machine deployments to modern, cloud-native technologies, such as containers and Kubernetes.
{svirt-long} provides a flexible platform that enables container and virtual machine workloads to run side-by-side, on the same, unified infrastructure.
Portworx Enterprise by {pxe-provider} brings resilient, scalable, and efficient storage services for mission-critical workloads, supporting enterprise availability, mobility, and data protection needs.
Together, {svirt-long} and Portworx by {pxe-provider} present enterprises with a robust, flexible infrastructure platform to support their mission-critical, virtualized and cloud-native workloads.

This guide provides an overview of this unified, infrastructure platform along with detailed steps for  configuring and validating the integration of {svirt-long} with Portworx Enterprise and a {pxe-provider} FlashArray to deliver a unified, infrastructure platform.

. *What problem does {svirt-long} with Portworx on bare metal solve?*
+
--
This solution addresses the challenges of running modern, stateful virtualized workloads with high availability, performance, and operational consistency.
It provides a unified platform to run virtual machines and containers together while ensuring enterprise-grade persistent storage, resiliency, and simplified lifecycle management.
--
. *Why deploy {svirt-long} on bare metal instead of a traditional hypervisor stack?*
+
--
Deploying on bare metal removes unnecessary abstraction layers, improves performance, and provides greater control over hardware resources.
It also enables tighter integration with Kubernetes, allowing organizations to manage virtual machines and containers using the same cloud-native operational model.
--
. *How does Portworx integrate with {svirt-long}?*
+
--
Portworx integrates as the persistent storage layer for {svirt-long} by providing block storage volumes to both virtual machines and container workloads.
It runs as a Kubernetes-native service and manages replication, failover, backup, and storage lifecycle operations transparently.
--
. *Is {pxe-provider} FlashArray required for this architecture?*
+
--
{pxe-provider} FlashArray is recommended and validated in this reference configuration due to its performance, reliability, and enterprise features.
However, Portworx can also work with other supported hardware storage arrays or local disks, provided they can present raw block devices to the {svirt-long} nodes.
--

. *What is the minimum cluster size for production deployments?*
+
--
Portworx requires a minimum of three storage nodes to establish quorum. For production environments, six storage nodes are recommended to improve fault tolerance, capacity distribution, and I/O performance during node failures or maintenance operations.
--

. *Can this architecture support both virtual machines and containers?*

+
--
Yes. {svirt-long} is designed to run virtual machines and Kubernetes workloads side by side on the same cluster.
Portworx provides persistent storage services for both workload types, enabling consistent data management across VMs and containers.
--

. *Is a dedicated storage network required?*
+
--
A dedicated storage network is strongly recommended for production environments.
Separating management and data traffic reduces contention and improves storage replication performance. For high-performance workloads, 25 Gbps or higher network bandwidth is preferred.
--
. *What are the recommended storage pool and backing disk configurations?*

+
--
Portworx recommends using a single backing disk per node to create the storage pool.
This approach simplifies capacity expansion, especially when using external storage arrays that support volume resizing, and avoids costly data rebalancing operations.
--
. *How does this architecture support future scalability and growth?*
+
--
The solution supports horizontal scaling by adding nodes and vertical scaling by expanding backing disks.
It also provides a foundation for hybrid cloud, edge deployments, and mixed VM/container workloads without requiring architectural changes.
--
. *Does this reference configuration support disaster recovery?*
+
--
Yes. Portworx provides built-in backup and disaster recovery capabilities. Advanced DR features, including synchronous replication across sites, are supported when integrated with an external etcd cluster and appropriate network connectivity.
--
. *Is this solution suitable for edge or remote deployments?*
+
--
Yes. {svirt-long} and Portworx are well-suited for edge environments due to their lightweight footprint, Kubernetes-native management, and ability to operate consistently across centralized data centers and remote locations.
--
